# Ablation Ratio

Here is the clearest way to read the ablation.

Matryoshka is doing what it is supposed to do: it is not mainly improving the model at near-full dimensionality, but making the embedding *more robust under aggressive truncation*. In your CSV, matryoshka beats the non-matryoshka version in 32 of 48 pairwise comparisons overall, but the effect is heavily concentrated at the smallest cuts: 11/12 wins at 64 dimensions, 9/12 at 128, 7/12 at 256, and only 5/12 at 512. Averaged over all models and tasks, the absolute gain is +0.0428 at 64 dims, +0.0193 at 128, +0.0020 at 256, and essentially neutral at 512 (-0.0012). That pattern is the main result: matryoshka is a *compression-robustness objective*, not a universal high-dimension booster. This matches the original goal of Matryoshka Representation Learning, which is to keep prefix-truncated embeddings useful across multiple operating points. ([arXiv][1])

The task-level story is also very clean. Retrieval is the strongest stress test and the one where matryoshka helps the most: the mean gain at 64 dims is +0.102, far above STS (+0.032), classification (+0.024), and reranking (+0.013). That suggests retrieval is the task most sensitive to how semantic information is distributed across coordinates, so it benefits the most when training explicitly forces early dimensions to remain informative. STS also benefits consistently, but the margins are smaller because the non-matryoshka baselines are already very strong under truncation, so there is a ceiling effect. Reranking is the least informative task here because most baselines are already near saturation even at 64 dimensions, leaving little room for matryoshka to help.

Model by model, the strongest evidence for the usefulness of matryoshka is **ModBERTBr**. Using the mean retention across 64/128/256/512 as a compact robustness index, ModBERTBr goes from 0.944 to 0.975 with matryoshka, which is by far the largest jump among the three families. The most striking individual result is retrieval at 64 dims: 0.620 to 0.835, a +0.215 absolute gain, or about +34.7% relative. It also improves STS and reranking consistently. This means that, for ModBERTBr, matryoshka is not just a small regularizer; it materially changes how much useful information survives in the leading dimensions.

The **multilingual e5-large** family shows the second clearest benefit. Its robustness index rises from 0.944 to 0.958. The gains are especially meaningful in retrieval (+0.068 at 64 dims) and classification (+0.036 at 64 dims), with smaller but still consistent gains in STS. The important point is that e5 and BERTimbau are both high-capacity 1024-dimensional encoders, yet e5 benefits much more from matryoshka than BERTimbau. So model size alone does not explain the effect. A plausible interpretation is that a multilingual encoder distributes semantic signal more diffusely because it must serve many languages and domains, so explicit dimension-wise supervision helps concentrate Portuguese-relevant information earlier in the vector. Taking your backbone as multilingual-e5-large, that model is initialized from XLM-R large, has 24 layers with 1024-dimensional embeddings, supports 100 languages, and inherits XLM-R’s SentencePiece tokenizer. ([Hugging Face][2])

**BERTimbau-large** shows the smallest matryoshka effect. Its robustness index moves only from 0.959 to 0.962. It improves at 64/128 dims in classification, retrieval, and STS, but the gains are modest, and reranking is slightly worse at all truncation levels. This is not a failure of matryoshka; it actually says something interesting about the baseline. BERTimbau already seems naturally robust to truncation, especially on STS, so there is less headroom for matryoshka to recover. In other words, the Portuguese-specialized monolingual BERT-Large baseline already packs semantic information relatively compactly, and matryoshka can only add a limited benefit. BERTimbau Large is a monolingual Brazilian Portuguese BERT-Large with 24 layers and 335M parameters, and its hidden size is 1024. ([Hugging Face][3])

A very important nuance for the paper is that **the results are not explained by “monolingual vs multilingual” alone**. Both BERTimbau and ModBERTBr are Portuguese-focused backbones, yet ModBERTBr gains much more from matryoshka. Likewise, both BERTimbau and e5 are 1024-dimensional, yet e5 gains more. That means the effect depends on how each backbone/tokenizer/representation geometry organizes information, not on a single binary architectural factor. For ModBERTBr specifically, your sentence-transformer checkpoint outputs 768-dimensional embeddings and is built on a ModernBERT backbone; the base model card also states that it uses a custom Unigram tokenizer. That makes it architecturally distinct from both BERTimbau and multilingual-e5. ([Hugging Face][4])

There is also one methodological caveat that is worth stating explicitly: your truncation grid uses **absolute dimensions**, but the full embedding size is not the same across families. BERTimbau and multilingual-e5 are 1024-dimensional, so 64/128/256/512 correspond to 6.25%/12.5%/25%/50% of the full vector. ModBERTBr is 768-dimensional, so the same cuts correspond to 8.3%/16.7%/33.3%/66.7%. Therefore, absolute cross-model comparisons slightly favor ModBERTBr because the compression is less severe. But this does **not** weaken the main conclusion, because the key comparison is within each family: matryoshka vs non-matryoshka under the same backbone. And under that fair comparison, ModBERTBr still shows the strongest gains. BERTimbau’s 1024-d shape is visible in its model card, multilingual-e5’s 1024-d shape is stated in its model card, and ModBERTBr’s sentence-transformer card reports 768-dimensional outputs. ([Hugging Face][3])

One more subtle point is that the small negative deltas at 256 or 512 for some models should not be over-interpreted. A few truncated settings are even slightly above 1.0, which indicates parity plus evaluation noise rather than a true “truncation beats full size” phenomenon. So the right interpretation is that matryoshka slightly reallocates representational capacity toward the earliest dimensions; that helps a lot when embeddings are aggressively compressed, and it costs little or nothing when dimensions are near full size. This is exactly the trade-off you would want in a deployment-oriented sentence encoder.

For the paper, I would emphasize these claims:

1. **Matryoshka mainly improves low-dimensional robustness, not full-dimensional quality.**
2. **The benefit is strongest for retrieval, so retrieval is the best task to showcase the effect.**
3. **The effect is architecture-dependent: it is very strong for ModBERTBr, moderate for multilingual-e5, and small for BERTimbau.**
4. **This variation cannot be reduced to monolingual vs multilingual or to model size alone.**
5. **Because the models have different full embedding sizes, cross-family comparisons should be discussed carefully, but within-family conclusions are strong.**

Yes — the absolute-score plots add an important layer that the ratio table alone cannot show. The ratio tells you how much of each model’s own maximum survives truncation; the absolute plots tell you which model is actually best at a given embedding budget. That distinction matters because a model can have a weaker retention ratio and still be better in absolute terms if matryoshka also raises the full-dimensional ceiling. The reranking plot is the clearest example: visually, the BERTimbau MRL curve stays above the non-MRL curve across the dimensional range, even though the ratio analysis alone makes the benefit look small or slightly unfavorable. The opposite kind of case also appears in STS: there, BERTimbau’s ratio can look better with matryoshka, but the non-MRL absolute curve remains slightly stronger through much of the medium/high-dimensional range. So one very good paper insight is that **matryoshka improves compression efficiency more consistently than it improves absolute score at every operating point**, and those two claims should be separated explicitly.

### Effect of Matryoshka Training Under Dimensional Truncation

We evaluate the impact of MatryoshkaLoss by analyzing both relative performance retention under dimensional truncation and absolute task performance across embedding sizes. The results show a clear and consistent pattern: the main contribution of matryoshka training is not to improve performance near the full embedding dimensionality, but to preserve performance under aggressive compression. Gains are concentrated at the smallest truncation levels, especially at 64 and 128 dimensions, and become progressively smaller as the embedding size approaches the original dimensionality. This indicates that MatryoshkaLoss primarily changes how information is distributed across the representation, encouraging the model to encode task-relevant semantic content earlier in the vector rather than relying on later dimensions.

This effect is most pronounced in retrieval. Among the evaluated tasks, retrieval shows the largest improvements under truncation, both in relative retention and in absolute performance. The matryoshka-trained models consistently outperform their non-matryoshka counterparts at low dimensionalities, with the largest margins appearing at the most aggressive truncation levels. This suggests that retrieval is particularly sensitive to the concentration of semantic information in the leading coordinates of the embedding, making it the task for which matryoshka training is most beneficial. STS exhibits a similar but weaker tendency: relative retention improves consistently, but the absolute gains are smaller, indicating that the corresponding baselines are already relatively robust to truncation. Classification shows intermediate behavior, with clearer benefits at low dimensionalities and smaller differences at larger ones. Reranking yields the most nuanced pattern, revealing that relative and absolute analyses capture complementary aspects of the effect. In some cases, matryoshka improves the absolute score at a given dimensionality while producing only marginal gains, or even slight decreases, in normalized retention. This occurs because retention is measured relative to each model’s own full-dimensional maximum; when matryoshka also improves that maximum, the normalized ratio may understate the practical benefit observed in absolute terms.

The model-wise results further show that the effect of matryoshka training is strongly architecture-dependent. ModBERTBr exhibits the largest gains under truncation, especially in retrieval, suggesting that its baseline representations are less naturally compact and benefit substantially from an explicit objective that forces useful information into the earliest dimensions. Multilingual E5 shows the second clearest benefit in robustness, while also achieving the strongest absolute performance on the most semantic tasks, particularly retrieval and STS, and becoming the strongest model in classification at moderate to high dimensionalities. BERTimbau, by contrast, shows the smallest relative gains from matryoshka training. This suggests that its monolingual Portuguese baseline is already comparatively compact, especially for STS, leaving less room for improvement through dimensional reallocation. Nevertheless, BERTimbau remains highly competitive in absolute terms, particularly in reranking and at low-dimensional classification settings, which indicates that Portuguese-specialized pretraining can already produce embeddings that are relatively stable under truncation even without explicit matryoshka supervision.

Importantly, these results do not support a simplistic monolingual-versus-multilingual interpretation. The multilingual model achieves the strongest absolute results on the most semantic tasks, showing that multilingual pretraining does not hinder Portuguese sentence representation quality and may even provide a richer semantic space for retrieval and similarity. At the same time, the monolingual BERTimbau model appears naturally more robust under truncation in some settings and remains competitive at smaller embedding budgets. ModBERTBr provides an important counterpoint: although it is also Portuguese-focused, it benefits much more from MatryoshkaLoss than BERTimbau. This indicates that the response to matryoshka training cannot be explained by language scope alone. Instead, it appears to depend on how each backbone distributes information across dimensions, which is likely shaped by a combination of architecture, tokenizer, pretraining data, and training objective.

Overall, the results support two main conclusions. First, MatryoshkaLoss should be understood primarily as a compression-oriented training objective: its main benefit is to preserve semantic effectiveness when embeddings must be truncated aggressively, rather than to improve performance when nearly all dimensions are retained. Second, the magnitude of this benefit is model-dependent. It is largest when the baseline encoder spreads useful information diffusely across dimensions and smaller when the baseline representation is already compact. From an application perspective, this makes matryoshka training especially attractive for deployment scenarios in which memory footprint, storage cost, or retrieval latency require low-dimensional sentence embeddings while maintaining as much downstream performance as possible.
